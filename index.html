Introducing

The C3PO (Complete Census of Co-moving Pairs Of stars) program observed a comprehensive sample of 125 comoving stellar twin pairs (250 stars) in the solar neighborhood. We utilized a combination of the Magellan Telescope, Keck Telescope, and the European Southern Observatory’s Very Large Telescope, totaling 15 days of telescope time from these 6-10m telescopes. The observations were conducted using high-resolution spectrographs, achieving resolving powers of R=40,000 to 110,000. The exposure times were carefully adjusted to ensure a typical signal-to-noise ratio of at least 200 per pixel.

By leveraging the stellar twin properties of the two stars in each pair, we performed a differential analysis, resulting in an 2% precision for over 30 elemental abundances.

Through this program, we aim to gain insights into the various astrophysical processes that can lead to chemical inhomogeneity between stars in a pair, such as planet engulfment, atomic diffusion, and inhomogeneities in star-forming gas clouds. The C3PO program has pushed the boundaries of our understanding of stellar chemistry and its connection to planetary systems and galactic evolution.

Key Results 1: Comoving Stars are Conatal

Yong, Liu, Ting, et al., 2023, MNRAS, 526, 2181

The figure above shows the relationship between spatial separation (Δs) and velocity separation (Δv) for the co-moving pairs of stars. The symbol sizes are scaled relative to the abundance difference between the two stars in a given pair. Chemically homogeneous and inhomogeneous co-moving pairs are denoted using blue and red symbols, respectively. Theoretical predictions for the conatal fraction from the Kamdar et al. (2019) simulations are shown in the background.

Our study, published in the Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society, provides evidence that comoving stars are indeed conatal, sharing the same birthplace and initial chemical composition.

We discovered that a spatial separation of Δs = 106 au (approximately 4.8 pc) serves as a boundary between chemically homogeneous and inhomogeneous pairs. For separations below this threshold, the majority of comoving pairs (70%) are chemically homogeneous, indicating a shared origin and conatal nature. In contrast, for separations larger than 106 au, only a minority of pairs (18%) exhibit chemical homogeneity, suggesting the presence of chance alignments or non-conatal associations.

We found no obvious trend between the fraction of chemically homogeneous pairs and their velocity separation (Δv). This finding confirms and extends the work of Kamdar et al. (2019), suggesting that the chemical homogeneity of comoving pairs is primarily determined by their spatial proximity rather than their kinematic properties.

These results have important implications for our understanding of star formation and the early evolution of stellar systems. By establishing the conatal nature of closely separated comoving stars, our study paves the way for using these systems as powerful tools to study phenomena that could lead to chemical inhomogeneity – including planet engulfment, which is the study in our subsequent paper below.


Key Results 2: At Least One in a Dozen Stars Engulfed Planetary Material

Liu, Ting, Yong, et al., 2024, Nature, 627, 501

The figure above illustrates the elemental abundance differences between the co-natal star pair. It highlights how the observed abundance data compares against various models that explain changes in elemental composition. The planetary engulfment model closely matches the observed data. This alignment suggests that one of the stars has ingested planetary material, affecting its chemical composition. The calculated Bayesian evidence, Δln(Z), indicates that the differences in chemical composition between these stars are best explained by the ingestion of planetary material, rather than by other processes such as atomic diffusion.

Our recent publication in Nature, featured on its cover, provides evidence for the widespread phenomenon of planetary material ingestion by stars. We found that approximately one in twelve pairs of co-natal stars exhibits this chemical inhomogeneity, indicating a notable frequency of planetary ingestion.

Enabled by high-resolution chemical analyses within the C3PO initiative, this research facilitated the detection of subtle differences in stellar chemical compositions.

Using a Bayesian analytical framework, we differentiated the signatures of planetary ingestion from other potential sources of chemical variation, such as atomic diffusion. We demonstrated that the observed chemical anomalies align most closely with scenarios of planetary ingestion.

These findings offer insights into the processes of planet formation and the subsequent dynamical interactions that may lead to planets being ingested by their host stars. These insights are critical for our understanding of the lifecycle of planetary systems and the mechanisms that may destabilize them.


Key Results 3: Lithium Enhancement from Planet Engulfment is Limited

[Sun, Ting, Liu, et al., 2024, ApJ, in press.]

The figure above shows theoretical predictions and observational evidence for lithium enhancement from planet engulfment. The left panel shows expectations for Earth-like planet ingestion, while the right panel shows Jupiter-like scenarios. Lavender points represent theoretical simulations of planet engulfment, while brown circles show observed co-natal pairs colored by age. Pairs with planet engulfment signatures (red circles) align well with theoretical predictions, demonstrating that lithium enhancement is typically modest and falls within the natural variation seen in stars of ±0.35 dex.

Our analysis of C3PO provides the largest homogeneous sample of high-precision lithium measurements in co-natal stars to date. Through detailed spectroscopic analysis, we discovered that even at fixed stellar parameters, stars exhibit an intrinsic scatter in lithium abundance of ±0.35 dex.

Most importantly, we found that the seven pairs previously identified with clear planet engulfment signatures show lithium differences that fall within this intrinsic scatter. We demonstrated that while planet engulfment can temporarily increase lithium levels, this enhancement is typically limited and short-lived before being depleted by stellar convection.

These findings challenge previous assumptions about using lithium as a primary indicator of planet engulfment. The study reveals that the natural variation in stellar lithium content, driven by factors such as magnetic activity, rotation, and early formation history, often masks any signature from planet engulfment events. This work emphasizes the importance of using comprehensive chemical abundance patterns, rather than lithium alone, when investigating planet engulfment scenarios.

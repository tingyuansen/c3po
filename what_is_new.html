Why Comoving Stars

Comoving stellar twins have emerged as an innovative tool in the study of planet formation and evolution. Historically, classical binary stars were the benchmark for such research. Yet, sufficiently luminous and proximate binaries to the Sun are rare, thus confining high-precision observations to a limited number of systems.

In contrast, comoving stars—those sharing the same velocity vector but potentially separated by up to 106 AU (whereas classical wide binaries are typically studied only up to 104 AU)—are much more abundant. Previously, the challenge of precisely measuring the positions and velocities of stars limited the identification of comoving pairs. This barrier has been surmounted by the European Gaia satellite, which provides detailed astrometric data for over a billion stars, allowing us to compile an extensive sample. This breakthrough has ushered us into a new era of research that deepens our understanding of planet formation.

Another critical challenge has been the uncertainty over whether comoving stars are truly analogous to binaries, specifically whether they originate from the same molecular cloud. Our research through the C3PO initiative and its preceding pilot study has conclusively demonstrated their co-natality, paving the way for this pioneering program.
